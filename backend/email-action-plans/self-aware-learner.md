## Your 3 Practices

These practices are designed to convert your awareness into instinct. You don't need more self-knowledge. You need reps that turn what you know into what you do automatically.

### **Practice 1: The Pre-Game Intention**

**What it is:**  
Before every date or significant interaction, choose one specific thing to practice. Not five things. One. "Tonight, I'm practicing staying present instead of future-projecting." Or: "Tonight, I'm practicing letting silences exist without filling them." Or: "Tonight, I'm practicing responding to what she says, not what my anxiety says."

**How to start:**  
Before your next date, pick your one intention based on what tripped you up last time. Write it on a sticky note or in your phone. Read it before you walk in. That's it. Don't try to be perfect across the board — that's a recipe for overthinking. Just practice one thing. After the date, assess: "How did I do on my one thing?" Not "How did the date go?" — that's about her. "How did I do on my practice?" — that's about you.

**Why it works for Self-Aware Learners:**  
You already know your patterns. The temptation is to try to fix all of them at once, which leads to overthinking and stiffness. A single intention focuses your practice and makes progress measurable. You'll improve faster by nailing one thing per date than by half-improving five things. And because you're self-aware, you'll naturally choose the right intention — the one that matches your most active blind spot.

---

### **Practice 2: The Real-Time Name-It**

**What it is:**  
When you feel an emotional reaction during a date — anxiety, comparison, insecurity, excitement that's too fast — name it internally in one word. Not "stop feeling this." Just: "Anxiety." "Comparison." "Rush." Naming the emotion creates a split-second of distance between feeling and reacting.

**How to start:**  
This week, practice in everyday situations first. Feel irritation in traffic? "Irritation." Feel jealousy scrolling social media? "Jealousy." Feel anxiety when a text takes too long? "Anxiety." Just the name, nothing else. Once this becomes automatic in low-stakes moments, it'll be available during dates. She mentions her ex and you feel a flash of comparison? "Comparison." She checks her phone and you feel anxiety? "Anxiety." The naming doesn't make the feeling disappear. It makes the feeling *visible* — and visible feelings don't run the show.

**Why it works for Self-Aware Learners:**  
Your awareness operates best in hindsight. You're great at the post-game — diagnosing what happened and why. This practice brings that awareness into real time. It's the bridge between "I know my patterns" and "I can catch them as they happen." For someone with your level of self-knowledge, just naming the emotion is often enough to prevent the reactive behavior. You already know what comes after "anxiety" — you just need to catch it before it arrives.

---

### **Practice 3: The Weekly Scenario Review**

**What it is:**  
Once a week, replay your most significant dating interaction from that week. Not to judge yourself — to study yourself. What signals did you read correctly? Where did emotion override awareness? What would you do differently if you could replay the moment?

**How to start:**  
Pick a consistent time — Sunday evening works well. Open a note on your phone. Write three sentences max: "This week I [what happened]. I noticed [what I did well]. Next time I'd [one adjustment]." That's the entire practice. Short. Focused. Growth-oriented. Don't turn it into a therapy session. Three sentences. If you want more structured scenarios to review — situations designed to test your specific blind spots — the Signal Theory app generates these for your profile. Think of it as a practice exam for the situations you know you'll face, with feedback that matches your Self-Aware Learner style.

**Why it works for Self-Aware Learners:**  
You learn from experience faster than anyone — but only if you process the experience deliberately. Without a structured review, your brain replays selectively (usually the anxiety-producing moments, not the whole picture). The weekly review gives you a balanced view: what worked, what didn't, what to practice next. Over time, these reviews compound. In a month, you'll have four weeks of pattern data that shows your trajectory — and Self-Aware Learners find that trajectory motivating. You're already improving. The review helps you see it.

## The Pattern to Watch For

Here's your early warning signal: **When you catch yourself thinking about your dating performance instead of the person in front of you.**

Self-Aware Learners have a unique pitfall: the meta-analysis trap. You're so focused on "Am I doing this right?" that you stop being present with the actual human across the table. You're grading yourself mid-conversation. You're monitoring your body language while she's telling you a story. You're running the checklist while she's trying to connect.

When you notice the inner monologue shift from "she's interesting" to "am I being interesting," that's your cue. Drop the self-monitoring. Come back to her. The paradox of self-improvement in dating is that the best moments happen when you forget you're trying to improve and just... show up.
