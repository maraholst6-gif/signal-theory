## What "The Inconsistent Dater" Looks Like

**In early dating (first 3-5 dates):**

You might find yourself texting a lot after a great first date — checking in, suggesting the next meetup, keeping the energy high. Then something shifts. Maybe she takes longer to respond. Maybe the second date wasn't as effortless as the first. Suddenly you pull back. You wait for her to initiate. You overthink every message. She goes from "he's interested" to "did I do something wrong?" in 48 hours. The confusion kills attraction.

Or the opposite happens: you're not sure how you feel after a date, so you go quiet. She interprets that as disinterest and moves on. Then three days later you realize you actually liked her — but by then, she's gone cold.

**In relationships (if you get there):**

The inconsistency continues. On good weeks, you're present, affectionate, making plans. On anxious weeks, you withdraw, need space, become hard to read. Your partner doesn't know which version of you to expect. It's not that you're playing games — you're just reacting to your internal state instead of responding to the situation.

**Your hidden strength:**

You're emotionally available. When you're engaged, you're genuinely engaged. You're not avoidant, not commitment-phobic, not playing power games. You just don't have a baseline approach yet. Once you get one, you'll be consistent in a way that most men aren't.

## Why This Pattern Exists

Most Inconsistent Daters developed this pattern because they never learned to separate *how they feel* from *what the situation warrants*.

In your last relationship, you probably didn't have to think about this. You had a rhythm. You knew what to do. But dating is different — every person is new, every situation is different, and there's no established pattern to fall back on. So you default to your mood.

When you're feeling good: chase. When you're feeling uncertain: retreat. When you're feeling confident: over-invest. When you're feeling anxious: under-invest.

This isn't weakness. It's just that you're responding to your internal weather instead of the actual signals in front of you. And internal weather is a terrible guide.

## Your 3 Practices

These practices are designed to give you a baseline approach that works regardless of how you feel. Think of them as your framework — so you stop winging it every time.

### **Practice 1: The 24-Hour Rule**

**What it is:**  
Before you text, suggest a date, or make any contact, ask yourself: "Am I doing this because the situation calls for it, or because I feel like doing it right now?"

If the answer is "because I feel like it," wait 24 hours. Then ask again.

**How to start:**  
This week, before you send any message to someone you're dating, pause. Set a 5-minute timer. Ask the question. If you're texting because you're excited/anxious/bored — don't send it yet. Come back to it tomorrow.

**Why it works for Inconsistent Daters:**  
Your pattern is mood-driven. This practice interrupts that. It doesn't mean never acting on feelings — it means not *only* acting on feelings. Over time, you'll start to notice the difference between "I want to text her because we had a good date and a check-in makes sense" vs "I want to text her because I'm anxious and need reassurance."

---

### **Practice 2: The Consistency Baseline**

**What it is:**  
Pick a baseline level of engagement and stick to it for the first 5 dates — regardless of how you feel.

Your baseline: One check-in text between dates. One date suggestion per week. No more, no less.

**How to start:**  
Write it down. Literally: "Between dates, I send one check-in text. I suggest one date per week." Put it in your phone notes. Refer to it when you're tempted to deviate.

**Why it works for Inconsistent Daters:**  
Right now, your engagement level swings based on mood. Some weeks you're texting constantly. Other weeks you go silent. Women can't read you because *you're* inconsistent. A baseline removes the guesswork — for you and for her. It's not rigid; it's reliable. And reliability builds trust.

---

### **Practice 3: The Post-Date Check-In (With Yourself)**

**What it is:**  
After every date, answer three questions before you decide how you feel:

1. Did I enjoy being around her?
2. Was there mutual interest (not just my interest)?
3. Do I want to see her again, or do I want the idea of seeing her again?

**How to start:**  
Keep a note in your phone labeled "Dating Check-Ins." After your next date, set a reminder for 24 hours later. When the reminder goes off, answer the three questions. One sentence each. Don't overthink it.

**Why it works for Inconsistent Daters:**  
Your pattern is to decide based on immediate feelings. Great date? You're all-in. Awkward moment? You're out. This practice creates space between the experience and the decision. It also helps you distinguish genuine interest from excitement/anxiety/loneliness — which all feel similar in the moment.

## The Pattern to Watch For

Here's your early warning signal: **When you catch yourself thinking "I should text her" or "I should give her space" — and you can't articulate *why* — that's your mood driving the decision, not the situation.**

Inconsistent Daters often confuse "what I feel like doing" with "what's calibrated." The goal isn't to never trust your gut. It's to know *when* your gut is reacting to the situation vs reacting to your own anxiety/excitement.

When you notice yourself about to text, pull back, suggest a date, or go quiet — pause and ask: "Is this based on what's actually happening between us, or is this based on how I feel right now?"

That pause is everything.

## What Inconsistent Daters Get Wrong About Themselves

Most Inconsistent Daters think they're "bad at dating" or "don't know what they want."

You're not bad at dating. You're reactive. And you *do* know what you want — you just don't trust it because it keeps changing based on your mood.

The fix isn't "figuring out what you want." It's building a framework so your behavior is consistent regardless of your mood. Calibrated doesn't mean emotionless. It means responsive to the situation, not just your internal state.

You're not broken. You just don't have a system yet.
